A Discounted Cash Flow, or DCF, model estimates what a company could be worth today by projecting future cash flows and then discounting those back to a present value. It focuses on the cash that could be available to shareholders rather than accounting earnings.
For Halliburton, the latest twelve month Free Cash Flow (FCF) is about $1.6b. Using a 2 Stage Free Cash Flow to Equity model, analysts provide explicit FCF estimates out to 2030, such as $1.9b for 2026 and $2.9b for 2030, and Simply Wall St extrapolates further years based on those inputs. All of these projected cash flows, expressed in $, are then discounted back to today and summed.
On this basis, the DCF model arrives at an estimated intrinsic value of $79.43 per share. Compared with the recent share price around $37.80, the model implies Halliburton trades at a 52.4% discount to this estimate. This outcome indicates the shares may be meaningfully undervalued according to these cash flow assumptions.

For profitable companies, the price to earnings, or P/E, ratio is a straightforward way to think about valuation, because it links what you pay for each share directly to the earnings that support that share. Investors usually expect a higher P/E when they see stronger earnings growth potential or lower business risk, and a lower P/E when growth expectations are more modest or risks feel higher.
Halliburton currently trades on a P/E of 24.6x. This sits below the Energy Services industry average of 26.8x and is slightly above the peer group average of 23.5x. Simply Wall St’s Fair Ratio for Halliburton is 26.4x. This is a proprietary estimate of what the P/E might be given factors such as earnings growth, profit margins, industry, market cap and company specific risks.
The Fair Ratio is more tailored than a simple comparison with peers or the broad industry, because it adjusts for the company’s own characteristics instead of assuming that all energy services names should trade on the same multiple. With the current P/E of 24.6x sitting below the Fair Ratio of 26.4x, this framework suggests the shares screen as undervalued on an earnings multiple basis.
