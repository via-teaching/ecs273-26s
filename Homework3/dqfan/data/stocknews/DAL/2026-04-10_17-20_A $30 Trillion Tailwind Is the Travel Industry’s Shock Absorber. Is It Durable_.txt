Inflation readings, oil volatility, and airline earnings: What to watch this week
Revenge travelwas a strong trend coming out of the pandemic, but there’s been a more structural change to U.S. travel in the interim — a $30 trillion tailwind.
On Wednesday, when Delta Air Lines reportedfirst-quarter earnings,CEO Ed Bastian offered what may be the most clarifying single statistic about the state of the travel industry in 2026.
“Our consumers, since Covid, have accumulated close to $30 trillion in incremental wealth,” Bastian said during a CNBC interview. “That’s households, as we define our consumer, of $100,000 or more in annual household earnings. And that’s 40% of the households in the country.”
While those 40% of households are somewhat buffered from rising gasoline prices and airfares from the Iran war and other inflationary impacts, the price spikes may force the other 60% of households to put off a summer vacation or to change it to a staycation.
“So there clearly is a component to the industry that is [in] trouble,” Bastian said. “And high oil prices for them are going to create a huge amount of duress.”
Bastian described his own company’s advantage — and the travel industry’s vulnerability at the same time. The $30 trillion tailwind is real. So are storm clouds for the other 60%.
High-income households have spent the five years since Covid seeing their home values rise, their equity portfolios expand, and their earnings grow. The total effect, across roughly 52 million U.S. households earning $100,000 or more, amounts to close to $30 trillion in net new wealth.
The impact is clear. Premium ticket revenue at Delta was up 14% in the first quarter. Bastian said he’s seen no dip in demand and bookings over the past 30 days were “the strongest period we’ve ever seen.”
From Delta’s perch, these are not the numbers of an industry bracing for demand destruction. They are the numbers of a consumer segment that has more money than it did five years ago and is spending it on travel.
This is why analysts at Barclays, Morgan Stanley, and Deutsche Bank have all namedHyatt their top hotel stock pick. Hyatt has built 18% of its rooms in luxury brands compared with just 2.4% at Hilton. And Hyatt’s Wall Street boosters are essentially making the same bet Bastian is: That the wealth accumulation is durable, and that the travelers who hold it will keep buying those premium seats and rooms.
Of course, if the stock market undergoes a major correction, then a portion of that $30 trillion benefit could be gone in a New York minute.
But Bastian also acknowledged the headwind, and that is where the travel industry’s real stress test is unfolding.
The 60% of U.S. households earning below $100,000 annually have been harder hit by inflation and many watched any pandemic-era savings erode. For them, the surge in oil prices since the start of the Iran war means higher fares and costs for roadtrips.
The U.S. Travel Association forecast that 2026 would be a big year for tax refunds to spur travel, butrefund amounts are falling short.
The impact is apparent across segments of the industry that serve the other 60%. Domestic hotel operators catering to middle-income leisure travelers are watching demand soften. Tour operators in the mid-market are seeing bookings slow. Budget and ultra-low-cost airlines, which lack Delta’s premium revenue base, are facing the same fuel costs but they have far less ability merely to pass them along to customers who were already price-conscious.
Bastian was candid about weaker airlines that can’t cope with the fuel price spike. “It’s going to separate the winners and force the weaker players to take some pretty significant steps to either get better or something else will happen.”
While not commenting on Delta’s appetite for mergers, Bastian said he expects further industry consolidation to take place.
The Iran war has not created the travel industry’s wealth divisions — it has accelerated and exposed them.
The geographic discrepancies in this disruption add more context. The Middle East’s hospitality sector has been severely disrupted. Tourism in the UAE and Qatar has plummeted, and the events industry has seen cancellations and bookings collapsing asinternational travelers abandon the region.
Travelers who once sought luxury and a cocoon from wider conflicts in the Middle East are pivoting to Europe and other destinations out of harm’s way.
Meanwhile, the World Cup, which is set to kick off in June across the U.S., Mexico, and Canada, is facing pressures. Hotels in some host cities are reportedlytracking below booking expectations,and the question of whether the World Cup demand bump will materialize as projected has become an open question.
A two-week ceasefire agreement announced April 7 offered some relief — airline stocks surged on the news. But whether it holds remains to be seen.
There is a caveat embedded in Bastian’s optimism about his own customers that the travel industry should not overlook. The $30 trillion in accumulated wealth is stored in 401(k) balances, home equity values, and brokerage accounts. The value of these assets sway with market health.
The high-income traveler who is currently immune to higher airfares is not protected from a world in which their stock portfolio is down 30% and the value of their real estate holdings plummet.
The travel companies that have restructured themselves almost entirely around that customer have made a bet on the durability of post-Covid wealth accumulation.
For now, the travel industry is operating in a business climate that fits in with Bastian’s positioning almost exactly: 40% of households providing high-margin demand; while 60% are trying to cut corners.
The companies that built for the 40% are outperforming. The companies that built for the 60% are under pressure. And the Iran war, whatever its ultimate resolution, is making that divide more stark.
