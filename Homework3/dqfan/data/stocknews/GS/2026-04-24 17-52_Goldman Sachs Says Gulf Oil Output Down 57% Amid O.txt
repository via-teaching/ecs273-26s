Oil markets are tightening at a pace that could start to reshape near-term expectations, as Goldman Sachs Group Inc. (NYSE:GS) points to a significant disruption across the Persian Gulf. The bank estimates regional crude output is running about 14.5 million barrels per day below pre-war levels this month, equivalent to a 57% decline following the outbreak of the Iran war. That scale of disruption suggests the supply shock is not isolated, but instead reflects a broader regional constraint that could take time to unwind.

Goldman analysts, including Daan Struyven, indicate that any recovery path may not be immediate. Their base case assumes a full and safe reopening of the Strait of Hormuz without renewed strikes, yet even under those conditions, a restoration of output would likely take a few months. The situation remains highly sensitive to developments in the strait, where traffic is still largely frozen as both Tehran and Washington attempt to enforce maritime blockades. The longer these curtailments persist, the more gradual the production recovery could become.
Price action is beginning to reflect that tightening backdrop. Brent crude has risen for a fifth straight session and is set for a weekly gain of 17%, with futures trading near $106 a barrel. That compares with levels below $73 before the war began, though still under the conflict peak of nearly $120. The move higher may indicate that markets are increasingly pricing in a prolonged disruption scenario, particularly if flows through the Strait of Hormuz remain constrained in the near term.
