Gladstone Capital(NASDAQ:GLAD) does not behave like a typical business development company. Where many BDC peers chase yield through aggressive leverage and equity-heavy portfolios, Gladstone Capital leans on secured debt, conservative leverage, and a lower middle market focus that has historically produced steadier returns than the headline yield alone suggests.
Gladstone Capital is a BDC, a structure that functions like a publicly traded private lender. It raises capital from investors, deploys it as loans to smaller private businesses, and is required by law to distribute at least 90% of taxable income to maintain its tax-advantaged pass-through status. Income comes primarily from interest on debt investments, supplemented by origination fees, prepayment penalties, and success fees.
Over 70% of debt investments at cost are secured first lien assets, meaning Gladstone Capital sits at the top of the repayment hierarchy if a borrower runs into trouble. That is structurally more defensive than BDCs that load up on subordinated debt or equity co-investments for higher yields. The tradeoff is a lower yield profile, but also a more durable one.
The key income metric for BDCs is not earnings per share butnet investment income (NII) per share, which captures cash interest and fees collected minus operating expenses. NII is what actually funds the monthly distribution.
The article’s title references a five-year total return advantage over the S&P 500. On a price-only basis: GLAD’s price has risen about 29% over the past five years, while the S&P 500 has gained about 65% over the same period. Any total return advantage over the index comes entirely from reinvested dividends, which at yields consistently above 10% can add meaningfully to the calculation. The price itself has significantly lagged the broader market.
More immediately, shares are down about 19% over the past year and off roughly 13% year-to-date. At a current price near $18, the annualized $1.80 dividend translates to a yield of roughly 10%. That yield is elevated in part because the stock has declined, a reminder that income and total return are not the same thing.
The dividend was cut. Gladstone Capital reduced its monthly common distribution from $0.165 to $0.15 per share starting in Q4 2025, a reduction driven by yield compression fromFederal Reserve rate cuts in late 2025.
Since the cut, coverage has been consistent. NII per share came in at $0.50 in Q1 2026, covering the $0.45 quarterly common distribution by a ratio of roughly 1.1 times. The same pattern held in prior quarters: Q4 2025 NII was $0.52 per share, Q3 2025 was $0.50, and Q2 2025 was $0.50, each covering the then-current distribution.

One data point worth flagging: a payout ratio of 113% has been cited by analysts, which raises sustainability questions. That figure uses trailing EPS rather than NII, and for BDCs, EPS includes unrealized gains and losses that are non-cash. NII coverage is the correct lens. The margin is thin enough that any further yield compression would put the distribution at risk again.
Gladstone Capital’s portfolio is predominantly floating-rate, so income rises and falls with SOFR. The weighted average yield on the interest-bearing portfolio has compressed from 13.9% in Q4 2024 to 12.2% in Q1 2026. The Fed has held rates at 3.75% since late 2025, which stops further compression but does not reverse it.
Management’s response has been to grow the loan book. The weighted average principal balance grew from $647.2 million in Q3 2025 to $772.3 million in Q1 2026, the primary lever keeping per-share NII stable as yields fall. Total investments at fair value reached $902.9 million as of December 31, 2025.
President Bob Marcotte stated after the fiscal year close: “FY25 closed on a high note as our lower middle market investment focus produced over $100 million of net originations, and we reset near term maturities and increased our floating rate funding via the debt refinancing concluded shortly after 9/30. These actions combined with our modest leverage have positioned us well to deliver continued asset growth and sustain our net interest income in support of future shareholder distributions.”
Gladstone Capital issued $149.5 million in 5.875% Convertible Notes due 2030, redeeming higher-cost 7.75% and 5.125% notes in a refinancing completed shortly after September 2025. That swap lowers interest expense and extends the debt maturity runway. The credit facility was also expanded to $320 million with a revolving period extended to October 2027 and final maturity to October 2029.
Portfolio quality is showing modest stress. Fair value as a percentage of cost has declined to 97.5% in Q1 2026 from 103.3% a year earlier. NAV per common share has drifted down from $21.41 to $21.13 over recent quarters. Neither figure is alarming in isolation, but the trend is worth monitoring. The stock currently trades at aprice-to-book ratioof about 0.84, meaning investors are buying the portfolio at a discount to stated book value.
The $0.15 monthly distribution is covered for now, but the margin is thin and the trajectory of key inputs (portfolio yield, NAV, fair value as a percentage of cost) is pointing downward. A second cut remains possible if loan book growth stalls or credit quality deteriorates further. Gladstone Capital makes sense for income investors who understand BDC mechanics, accept that total return depends heavily on reinvested dividends, and are comfortable with a distribution that was already reduced once and could be again if loan book growth stalls or credit quality deteriorates. Price appreciation has not been the story here, and the yield carries real compression risk.
