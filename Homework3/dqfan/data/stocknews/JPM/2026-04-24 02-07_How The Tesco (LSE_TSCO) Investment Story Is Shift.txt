Tesco’s fair value estimate in recent analyst models now sits at £5.10, compared with £4.89 previously, putting a fresh spotlight on how price targets are being recalibrated. Recent updates from large banks, including a 20 GBp move at JPMorgan and a 10 GBp change at Deutsche Bank, show how both confidence and caution are being reflected in these revised targets. Read on to see what is driving this evolving narrative and how you can keep track of the shifting assumptions behind it.

JPMorgan lifted its Tesco price target by 20 GBp, which points to increased conviction in the company’s ability to execute on its current plan and support the revised fair value estimate around £5.10.
Deutsche Bank raised its Tesco price target by 10 GBp, adding another supportive data point for investors tracking how large banks are reassessing the risk and reward trade off in the shares.
Citi increased its Tesco price target by €30, signalling that its analysts see enough progress and visibility in the story to justify a higher valuation anchor.
Even with higher price targets from JPMorgan, Deutsche Bank and Citi, the changes still reflect a measured approach, with analysts refining assumptions rather than making wholesale upgrades.
The clustering of target moves in relatively tight increments suggests that large banks remain focused on execution risks, and investors may want to pay close attention to how future updates align with the current £5.10 fair value estimate.

Tesco’s Board of Directors authorized a share buyback plan on April 16, 2026, signalling an intention to return capital to shareholders over time.
The company announced a share repurchase program of up to £750 million, with purchases planned to run until April 2027, which provides a clear sense of the scale and timeline of the buybacks.
Tesco’s planned buyback activity provides an additional data point for investors tracking how the company allocates excess capital, alongside other potential uses such as dividends or reinvestment.
