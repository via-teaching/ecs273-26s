Earlier this week, oilfield services heavyweight Halliburton reported past first-quarter profits that exceeded Wall Street expectations, supported by stronger international demand across Latin America, Europe and Africa, which lifted sentiment across the broader services sector including smaller peer RPC.
The read-through for investors is that improving activity in overseas markets is influencing expectations for North American-focused providers like RPC, even though its operations remain largely regional.
We’ll now examine how stronger international demand highlighted by Halliburton’s results might influence RPC’s existing investment narrative and risk-reward profile.

To own RPC, you need to be comfortable with a regional, North American oilfield services business whose fortunes are closely tied to pressure pumping and wireline activity, particularly in the Permian. Halliburton’s stronger international results may lift sentiment around service demand, but they do not materially change RPC’s near term catalyst of stabilizing margins or its key risk of pricing pressure and intense competition in its core basins.
The most relevant recent disclosure against this backdrop is RPC’s 2025 results, which showed full year revenue of about US$1,626.6 million and net income of roughly US$32.1 million, down from the prior year. Those figures highlight the tension between top line growth and margin compression at a time when sector optimism is improving, and they frame how investors might weigh any benefit from stronger global activity against ongoing pricing and cost risks for RPC.
