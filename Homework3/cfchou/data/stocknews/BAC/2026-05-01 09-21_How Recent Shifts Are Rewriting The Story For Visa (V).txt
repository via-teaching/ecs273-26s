The latest analyst update nudged Visa model fair value estimate from US$396.83 to US$395.71, signaling only a very small adjustment to where the shares are pegged in updated work. That modest shift lines up with a research backdrop where some firms are lifting targets or highlighting Visa as a core payments holding, while others are trimming price targets and stressing valuation, growth resilience, regulation and new payment options. As you read on, you will see how these crosscurrents are shaping the evolving narrative around Visa and what to watch next. Analyst Price Targets don't always capture the full story. BofA reinstated coverage of Visa with a Buy rating and a US$410 price target and also added the stock to its "US 1 List," signaling that its analysts see Visa as one of their preferred ideas among Buy rated U.S. names. BMO Capital and Loop Capital both initiated Visa with bullish views, and Daiwa and Freedom Capital upgraded the shares, with Freedom Capital highlighting that Visa is priced lower than Mastercard on its view and could see a re rating if it sustains stronger relative growth. Truist reported that buy side investors in its survey picked Visa among the top names they would be prepared to defend within financials, grouping it with large card issuers and brokers. Citi cut its Visa price target by US$50, while Evercore ISI, UBS and Truist also lowered targets, signaling that some analysts are reassessing valuation and risk reward even while ratings may remain positive. Truist flagged that its consumer finance coverage is "flirting with bear market territory," with concerns around rate caps, AI driven unemployment fears and potential shifts to off network spend weighing on sentiment toward card related names such as Visa. SoftBank's PayPay is preparing a U.S. Nasdaq listing targeting up to a US$14b valuation, with Visa among cornerstone investors expected to commit more than US$200m to the IPO. Visa and PayPay agreed a broad partnership to expand PayPay's services into the U.S. and deepen cooperation in Japan, covering QR code acceptance, card payments and cross border use cases. Mexico's antitrust regulator rejected a proposed Visa takeover of local processor Prosa, citing concerns about higher market concentration and potential risks to the payments system. Visa announced a share repurchase program authorizing up to US$20,000m in class A buybacks. It later disclosed that 50,828,542 shares were repurchased for US$16,731m under the plan announced in April 2025. Fair value estimate shifted from US$396.83 to US$395.71.
