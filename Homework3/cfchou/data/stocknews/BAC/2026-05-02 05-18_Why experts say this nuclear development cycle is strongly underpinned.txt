Title: Why experts say this nuclear development cycle is strongly underpinned
Date: 2026-05-02 05:18
URL: https://finance.yahoo.com/sectors/energy/articles/why-experts-nuclear-development-cycle-051825891.html?.tsrc=rss
Content:
Investing.com --Bank of America (BofA) Global Research indicates that the nuclear fuel market is in the early stages of a multi-decade development cycle characterized by shifting utility behavior and tightening physical supply. Analysts suggest this "third nuclear build wave" is fundamentally different from previous cycles, underpinned by energy security, decarbonization, and electrical intensification. BofA forecasts a strong uranium price outlook for the second half of 2026 and into 2027, with prices expected to average $135/lb, a 56% increase over current spot pricing. The primary challenge facing the market is a lack of replacement production. Existing mines are aging, and new projects face long timelines, leaving the market structurally exposed to deficits. Utilities are reversing a decade-long trend of drawing down inventories by actively contracting and prioritizing security of supply. The latest shift is illustrated by a willingness to contract at higher price points, with buyers recalibrating expectations toward the $100/lb level. Supply response remains slow as incremental production, particularly from in-situ recovery (ISR), becomes structurally higher in cost. Furthermore, "stealth" demand inflation is occurring as utilities extend refueling cycles and enrichment facilities require more natural uranium to offset higher enrichment costs. Analysts argue that 2026 will be a pivotal year for uranium contracting, marking a shift where utilities may over-contract to rebuild strategic reserves. Investment demand has tightened physical supply, with approximately $9 billion in uranium sequestered in closed-ended vehicles. Such capital flows effectively remove material from circulation indefinitely. Despite potential short-term pullbacks due to macro volatility, nuclear demand remains "sticky" because reactors are baseload assets that do not easily shut down during economic downturns. Geopolitical risks further influence the sector, with recent conflicts highlighting the fragility of energy systems dependent on fossil fuels. Supply chain disruptions, such as the global sulfur shortage required for leaching, underscore the vulnerability of mine supply. Consequently, the incentive price required for new production is expected to move materially higher.
