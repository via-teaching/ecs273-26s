If you are wondering whether Johnson & Johnson is still reasonably priced at around US$227.19, you are not alone, especially after such a strong run in recent years. The stock has recorded a 49.6% return over the last year and 9.6% year to date, even as the share price has eased with a 6.9% decline over the past month and a small 0.1% decline over the last week. Recently, investor attention has focused on Johnson & Johnson's position as a large global healthcare group, with ongoing headlines around product litigation, regulatory scrutiny and its portfolio mix between pharmaceuticals, medical devices and consumer health. These themes help frame why the share price has been strong over the last year but softer in the shorter term, as investors weigh long term brand strength against legal and regulatory risks. A Discounted Cash Flow model takes projected future cash flows, then discounts them back to today using a required rate of return, to estimate what the business might be worth now on a cash basis. For Johnson & Johnson, the model used is a 2 Stage Free Cash Flow to Equity approach. The latest twelve month free cash flow is about $17.0b. Analysts have provided forecasts out to 2030, with free cash flow projections such as $26.5b in 2026 and $35.9b in 2030. Beyond the explicit analyst period, Simply Wall St extrapolates further cash flows using its own growth assumptions. When all those projected cash flows are discounted back to today, the DCF model arrives at an estimated intrinsic value of about $376.56 per share. Compared with the current share price of around $227.19, this implies a 39.7% discount. This points to the shares trading below this model based estimate of value. For profitable companies like Johnson & Johnson, the P/E ratio is a common way to think about value because it links what you pay for each share to the earnings that the business is already generating. A higher or lower P/E usually reflects what the market expects for future earnings growth and how much risk investors see in those earnings. Johnson & Johnson currently trades on a P/E of 26.0x. This sits above the Pharmaceuticals industry average of 17.0x and slightly above the peer group average of 25.2x. On the surface that points to investors paying a premium compared with the wider industry and with similar companies. Simply Wall St also calculates a proprietary “Fair Ratio” for each stock, which for Johnson & Johnson is 28.5x. This Fair Ratio is designed to be more tailored than a simple peer or industry comparison because it blends factors such as earnings growth, profit margins, company size, industry characteristics and specific risk profile. Comparing the current P/E of 26.0x with the Fair Ratio of 28.5x suggests the shares are trading below this model based view of a fair earnings multiple.
