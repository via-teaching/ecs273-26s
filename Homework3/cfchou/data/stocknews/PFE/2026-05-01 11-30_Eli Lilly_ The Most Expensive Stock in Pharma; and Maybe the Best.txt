Title: Eli Lilly: The Most Expensive Stock in Pharma; and Maybe the Best
Date: 2026-05-01 11:30

It isn't often that a nearly $1 trillion company moves with the volatility of a speculative biotech, but that is exactly what I am seeing today as Eli Lilly drops its Q1 2026 results. The stock has surged by about 8% in a single session, finally reclaiming its momentum after a choppy few months where it retreated from its $1,075 highs to below $900. Today, at roughly $940 per share, Lilly has effectively decoupled from the rest of the Big Pharma pack. If you look back just three and a half years, Lilly is up a staggering 245%, while its peer Pfizer has plummeted by 50% in that same window. We are witnessing a total divergence in the sector, where Lilly is trading like a Silicon Valley growth story rather than a traditional drug maker. Warning! LLYData byGuruFocus The mood in the market has shifted from questioning the bubble to acknowledging that the perfection priced into this stock is actually showing up in the quarterly ledger. Lilly is clearly winning the GLP-1 race. In Q1 2026, revenue jumped a staggering 56% to $19.8 billion, which blew past the mean analyst estimate of $17.82 billion. When a company at this scale produces that kind of growth, the valuation starts to look less like a risk. The Q1 2026 earnings shock has essentially validated my long-standing belief that Lilly is in a class of its own. Beyond the revenue beat, the bottom line was even more impressive, with non-GAAP earnings per share (EPS) hitting $8.26; a 170% increase year-over-year. What stands out to me is that Lilly achieved this while including a $0.52 write-down for in-process research and development. Management has responded to this strength by raising full-year 2026 guidance across the board. They now expect revenue to land between $82 billion and $85 billion, up from the previous range of $80 billion to $83 billion. At the midpoint, this reflects 28% year-on-year growth. On the earnings side, they are now forecasting EPS between $35.50 and $37.00. This tells me that the cooling off period I previously worried about is being pushed further out into the future as the manufacturing capacity finally begins to catch up with the global demand. Net income for the quarter increased 155% to $7.66 billion. This level of profitability for a healthcare firm is remarkable and suggests that as volume scales, the company is enjoying massive operating leverage. Only a handful of companies have ever achieved this level of growth at this scale; it reminds me more of a high-end tech firm during a hardware cycle than a pharmaceutical company. Lilly has officially moved beyond being a firm that makes drugs; it is now a global utility for cardiometabolic health. To understand how we got to this Q1 breakout, we have to look back at the foundation laid in 2025. The company reported total revenue of $65.2 billion for 2025, which comfortably sailed past its own upwardly revised guidance of $63 to $63.5 billion. In the fourth quarter of 2025 alone, revenue hit $19.3 billion, a 43% year-over-year jump. This tells me that the demand for Mounjaro and Zepbound is definitely accelerating. The profitability metrics from that period were equally impressive. Non-GAAP EPS for 2025 came in at $24.21, beating the high end of the company's guidance by a significant margin. If we look at the fourth quarter of 2025 specifically, EPS hit $7.54, which was a 42% increase over the same period the previous year. What stood out to me then (and remains true now) is that Lilly managed this growth despite incurring substantial costs, including recurring charges for acquired in-process research and development. LLYData byGuruFocus Maintaining these margins is no small feat. The gross margin as a percentage of revenue rose slightly to 82.5% in the final quarter of 2025. This indicates that while the company is aggressively expanding its manufacturing footprint and facing some pricing pressure, it is successfully neutralizing those headwinds through product mix improvements and economies of scale. From my perspective, this is a company that has moved beyond the experimental phase of a product launch and into a high-efficiency harvesting phase, even as it continues to innovate. In my view, the real moat protecting Eli Lilly isn't just a patent; it's a factory. The battle for the GLP-1 market has shifted from who has the best molecule to who can actually put that molecule into a vial and get it onto a pharmacy shelf. While its chief rival, Novo Nordisk, has struggled with significant manufacturing bottlenecks and complications, Lilly has been playing a much more proactive game. The company has now committed roughly $50 billion to building out its manufacturing sites, primarily in the U.S. This level of capital expenditure is a clear signal that management expects the current demand surge to last for a decade, not just a few quarters. This manufacturing edge is already showing up in the market share data. By the end of Q1 2026, Lilly had captured over 60% of the U.S. GLP-1 prescription market. This is a dramatic shift from just two years ago when the market was more evenly split. The data suggests a widening growth gap between the two companies. While Lilly's revenue grew by 56% in the recent quarter, Novo Nordisk's stock has fallen nearly 40% on a 12-month basis due to these operational difficulties. I believe Lilly is effectively turning the obesity market into a monopoly or, at the very least, a lopsided duopoly where it holds all the operational advantages. Furthermore, these manufacturing investments align Lilly with domestic policy priorities. By building at home, Lilly has secured some favorable nation drug pricing policies and an exemption from certain aggressive price caps. It's an end-to-end strategy that makes the business much more resilient to shifts in how pharmacy benefit managers or traditional retailers behave. If you can control the supply chain at a $50 billion scale, you are the market. If there is a concern for an analyst, it is usually pricing erosion. In the latter half of 2025, realized prices for Lilly's products in the U.S. fell by about 7% as the company aggressively pursued volume. On the surface, falling prices are usually a red flag. However, when I dig into the volume data, the story changes. That drop in price has been consistently offset by massive increases in volume; 50% or more in recent quarters. I don't see these price moves as a sign of weakness. By lowering the entry price for Zepbound and Mounjaro (sometimes to as little as $350 per month through specific agreements) Lilly is making it much harder for competitors to gain a foothold. They are effectively trading a bit of margin today for a dominant market share tomorrow. Management's guidance for 2026 suggests they expect this trend to continue, and the recent Q1 results confirm that the volume uplift is still far outstripping any price concessions. We also have a major new tailwind on the regulatory front. The FDA recently ruled that tirzepatide (the molecule in Mounjaro and Zepbound) will be excluded from the 530B bulks list. This means that compounding facilities and outsourcing firms can no longer produce compounded or generic versions of these drugs. This shuts down a major loophole that was being used by various companies to undercut Lilly's pricing. By forcing the market back to the official FDA-approved product, Lilly has essentially secured its revenue stream from a significant threat. The next frontier for Lilly has already arrived: the transition from needles to pills. While injectables are currently the gold standard, the injection barrier prevents many patients from starting therapy. This is why the launch of Foundayo (orforglipron), Lilly's oral GLP-1 candidate, is so critical. Recently, it was reported that Foundayo has secured approval and is already seeing a rollout. Early data suggests it achieved approximately 3,700 prescriptions in its second week on the market. While this is a slower start than Novo's Wegovy pill, I expect it to be a massive seller over the long term. Foundayo has a significant convenience advantage. Unlike Novo's oral version, which has restrictive fasting and water requirements, Foundayo can be taken at any time of day without food or water restrictions. Data suggests it can achieve a mean weight reduction of about 12.4%, and while some competitors might claim slightly higher percentages, the ease of use is what will drive adherence. Analysts are already speculating that Foundayo could earn $1.6 billion in 2026 alone, with peak revenues eventually crossing the $25 billion mark. Lilly is essentially competing against its own successful injectable products with this launch, but that is exactly what a market leader should do. By developing their own oral replacements, they are ensuring that even when the patents for Mounjaro expire in the 2030s, the company will already have moved the market to the next generation of superior therapies. It's a strategy of self-disruption that protects their pre-eminence in the field. Looking beyond the current blockbusters, Lilly is diversifying its bets. Besides the cardiometabolic division, which earned $15.76 billion in Q1, the company has significant footholds in other areas. Its oncology division earned $2.3 billion, immunology brought in $1.2 billion, and neuroscience added $382 million. While these look small compared to the obesity numbers, they are substantial businesses in their own right. The oncology drug olomorasib and immunology candidates like simepdekinra could add up to another $100 billion in value over time. Lilly is using its massive income generation (which was roughly $20.6 billion in 2025) to fund a targeted M&A spree. Lilly prefers early-stage, high-science deals. Recent examples include the $2.4 billion acquisition of Orna Therapeutics, a $7 billion deal for Kelonia, and a $6.3 billion contingent value deal for Centessa Pharmaceuticals. They also added Ajax Therapeutics for $2.3 billion. These moves into in-vivo gene therapy and narcolepsy show that management is thinking about the world after the obesity boom. The most exciting candidate in the pipeline right now is retatrutide, often called the "Triple G" because it targets three receptors. In studies, it achieved A1C reductions of up to 2% and weight loss of over 16% at 40 weeks in patients with type 2 diabetes. This drug could potentially expand Lilly's reach into cardiovascular outcomes and even alcohol use disorder. The fact that Lilly is not resting on its laurels but is actively building a pipeline that could obsolete its own current leaders is a sign of a very healthy R&D culture. Valuing Eli Lilly right now is a challenge because it moves more like a growth stock than a value stock. At $940 per share, the stock trades at roughly 25x forward earnings based on the new 2026 guidance. While that is a premium to the sector median, it is actually lower than Lilly's own five-year average forward P/E of around 39x. I believe the operational outperformance justifies this premium. If we assume a 3-5 year view, the question is what return an investor is underwriting at these levels. LLYData byGuruFocus For me to justify a Buy rating at $920, I have to see a path to a $1 trillion valuation and beyond. If the company hits its goal of breaking the $100 billion per annum revenue mark by 2028; which seems likely given the current 56% growth rate; the overvalued tag starts to look outdated. Even if the P/E multiple contracts as the company matures, the explosive earnings growth should drive a healthy double-digit annual return for shareholders over the next several years. The risk, of course, is a multiple contraction. If the market decides that 25x is too high and re-rates Lilly closer to the sector average of 19x, the stock price would suffer even if the earnings are perfect. We saw this with Novo Nordisk, which shed a massive amount of value when clinical and regulatory challenges hit. Lilly is trading with very little margin of safety right now, but when you have 80% gross margins and a market share that is growing rather than shrinking, you are often rewarded for paying up for quality. It's always instructive to see where the gurus are placing their bets. As of late 2025 and early 2026, institutional interest in Eli Lilly remains exceptionally high.PRIMECAP Management(Trades,Portfolio) remains a massive anchor for the stock, andKen Fisher(Trades,Portfolio)'s firm has continued to hold its significant position. What interests me more is the aggressive buying we've seen from hedge fund elites likeRay Dalio(Trades,Portfolio) and Steve Cohen. Dalio's firm increased its stake by over 600%, while Cohen's firm ramped up by over 800%. While some funds have trimmed their positions to manage their total asset weight, the entry of new major players likeRenaissance Technologies(Trades,Portfolio) andPaul Tudor Jones(Trades,Portfolio) suggests that the professional money still views Lilly as a core holding for the next growth cycle. I also note the "yield on cost" for long-term holders. For those who bought a decade ago, the yield on cost is now over 8%. Even though the current forward yield is 0.6%, the company's history of consistent dividend growth makes it an attractive long-term play. The dividend payout ratio is less than 1%, which gives management massive flexibility to either continue their M&A spree or eventually return a lot more cash to shareholders once the factory build-out is complete. Eli Lilly has cemented its status as the world's leading pharmaceutical company. The Q1 2026 earnings significantly beat expectations. Between the tirzepatide franchise on track to earn $50 billion this year and the successful rollout of Foundayo providing a new convenience moat, Lilly has effectively locked down the most lucrative segment of the global healthcare market. While there are psychological barriers to investing at these high prices, the fundamentals tell a story of continued outperformance. The $50 billion manufacturing investment and the deep pipeline of candidates like retatrutide ensure that Lilly is not a one-trick pony. The bear case has always been that it is "too late to invest," yet the stock has continued to climb, up 400% over the last five years. I am retaining a Buy rating on Eli Lilly. The positives; specifically the 56% revenue growth and the 60% market share; far outweigh the concerns about valuation. For the investor with a 3-5 year horizon, I expect the company to reclaim its $1,075 highs and trend toward my $1,200 target. The bandwagon is moving fast, but given the results we saw today, there is still plenty of room to climb aboard.
