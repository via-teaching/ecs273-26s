Corning (NYSE:GLW) and Meta have started a $6b expansion of Corning's optical cable plant in North Carolina. The project is intended to support AI data center growth and strengthen US based fiber supply. The expansion represents a multi year commitment between Corning and a large technology partner focused on AI infrastructure. Corning, trading at $158.26, has seen very strong share price performance, with the stock up 74.5% year to date and 251.1% over the past year. The new expansion with Meta adds a large, tangible project on top of existing long term agreements around generative AI and fiber demand, reinforcing the role of Corning's optical segment in current market themes. For investors tracking NYSE:GLW, this development ties Corning's growth more closely to the build out of AI data centers and high capacity networks. The multi year nature of the project may be important when assessing how the company is positioning its manufacturing footprint and supply chain inside the US for future demand cycles in AI infrastructure. The Meta expansion sits right in the middle of what you have already seen in Corning’s recent numbers. Q1 2026 sales were US$4,144m versus US$3,452m a year earlier, with Optical Communications called out as a key contributor, and the company has now broken ground on a US$6b optical cable project with Meta in North Carolina. Having Meta as an anchor customer ties Corning more closely to AI data-center build outs, alongside peers like Ciena and Cisco that are also targeting high capacity networking. At the same time, Corning has kept its quarterly dividend at US$0.28 per share and filed a universal shelf registration that gives it flexibility to fund large projects like this with equity, debt, or other securities if needed. For you, the combination of a large multi year plant build, solid recent earnings, and access to capital matters because it frames how Corning might balance growth in AI focused fiber against capital intensity, customer concentration, and the risks already flagged around valuation and insider selling. The Meta deal and US$6b plant expansion align with the narrative view that AI data centers and optical-fiber demand can be central drivers for Corning’s Springboard plan and its focus on Optical Communications. Relying on a few hyperscale customers for multi year capacity, including Meta, reinforces the narrative concern that concentrated demand and timing of AI build outs could challenge profitability if large projects slow or are rephased. The specific decision to expand U.S. manufacturing in North Carolina at this scale, with Meta as an anchor customer, goes further than the narrative’s general discussion of U.S. footprint and may not be fully reflected in earlier scenario work.
