If you are wondering whether Pfizer at around US$27.47 is a bargain or a value trap, the key is understanding what the current price actually implies about the company.
The stock is up 9.1% year to date and 31.0% over the last year, even though it shows a 3.8% decline over the past week and a 2.5% gain over the past month. These moves can change how the market views both its potential and its risks.
Recent headlines have focused on Pfizer's shifting pipeline mix, product portfolio updates, and the market's reassessment of large pharmaceutical names. This helps frame why the share price has moved the way it has. This backdrop matters because it shapes investor expectations that will feed into any valuation work you do.
On Simply Wall St's valuation checks, Pfizer currently scores 3 out of 6. Next you will see how different valuation approaches line up on this, along with a more complete way to think about what the stock might be worth by the end of the article.

A Discounted Cash Flow, or DCF, model takes projected future cash flows and then discounts them back to today using a required rate of return to estimate what the whole company could be worth right now.
For Pfizer, the latest twelve month Free Cash Flow (FCF) is about $8.5b. Analysts provide explicit forecasts for several years, and Simply Wall St then extends these to build a longer 2 stage Free Cash Flow to Equity model. In this framework, Pfizer's projected FCF in 2030 is $16.1b, with intermediate annual figures for 2026 to 2035 ranging between roughly $15.8b and $20.1b before discounting. All of these cash flows are in $.
After discounting those projected cash flows back to today, the DCF model produces an estimated intrinsic value of $63.69 per share. Compared with the current share price around $27.47, the model implies the stock is 56.9% undervalued on this basis.

For a profitable company, the P/E ratio is a useful way to link what you pay per share to the earnings that each share currently generates. It helps you see how many dollars investors are willing to pay today for one dollar of current earnings.
What counts as a normal or fair P/E depends on how the market views a company's growth prospects and risk. Higher expected growth or lower perceived risk can justify a higher P/E, while slower growth or higher uncertainty can point to a lower one.
Pfizer currently trades on a P/E of 20.17x. That is above the Pharmaceuticals industry average of 16.88x and also above the peer average of 18.60x. Simply Wall St's Fair Ratio for Pfizer is 20.65x, which is a proprietary estimate of what Pfizer's P/E might be given its earnings profile, industry, profit margins, market cap and risk factors.
This Fair Ratio can be more useful for you than a simple peer or industry comparison, because it adjusts for company specific traits rather than assuming all drug makers deserve the same multiple.
With a current P/E of 20.17x compared to a Fair Ratio of 20.65x, Pfizer screens as slightly undervalued on this metric.
