Exxon Mobil (NYSE:XOM) is heading into its first-quarter print with a growing disconnect between operational disruption and commodity tailwinds, as the Iran war begins to show up directly in both production and earnings visibility. The company disclosed that roughly 6% of global output was lost during the quarter as oil and natural gas operations across the Persian Gulf were paralyzed, with about half of that impact tied to a liquefied natural gas complex in Qatar. Two LNG trains at the facility were damaged by Iranian missile strikes, and Exxon indicated repairs could take a prolonged period, with no clear timeline until an on-site evaluation is completed, leaving a key portion of its regional capacity effectively offline for now.

The earnings setup looks more nuanced than the headline disruption suggests. Exxon expects a $3.7 billion sequential decline in its energy-products division, reflecting price volatility and cargo timing effects during the quarter, dynamics that management framed as temporary and potentially reversing into material profits once underlying trades are completed. At the same time, higher commodity prices are providing a counterbalance, with the company estimating gains of about $2.1 billion from crude and $400 million from natural gas. Excluding timing effects, per-share earnings were higher quarter over quarter, reinforcing the idea that core profitability may still be holding up despite the operational hit.
The broader signal for investors could be the shift in how the Gulf is being priced as an energy hub. The region typically accounts for around one-fifth of Exxon's global output, and the conflict appears to be challenging assumptions around stability and investability, with early signs already reflected in share price weakness ahead of trading following the ceasefire announced by President Donald Trump. Exxon's update also aligns with similar commentary from peers pointing to lower gas production, suggesting the supply disruption could be more widespread and possibly longer-lasting than initially expected, even as Exxon continues to scale output through recent growth projects and acquisitions that have lifted production by more than 30% over the past three years.
