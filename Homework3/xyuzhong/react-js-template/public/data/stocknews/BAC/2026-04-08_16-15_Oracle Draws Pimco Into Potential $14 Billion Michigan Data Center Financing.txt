Oracle (NYSE:ORCL) is seeing renewed momentum in its data center financing push, with Pacific Investment Management Co. now in early discussions with Bank of America (NYSE:BAC) to help arrange roughly $14 billion in debt for a large-scale Michigan campus tied to OpenAI workloads. The talks, while still preliminary, could position Pimco as a central financing partner, with the structure potentially taking the form of a bond that may later be syndicated to institutional investors. The project in Saline Township, led by Related Digital and coordinated by Bank of America, has already drawn sustained attention across Wall Street, as investors weigh both the scale and long-term demand profile tied to AI infrastructure.

The financing package could be executed through a 144A private placement, allowing the debt to be distributed among qualified institutional buyers, a structure often used for transactions of this size and complexity. Blackstone is also expected to contribute an additional $2 billion in equity, reinforcing the broader capital stack supporting the development. For Pimco, participation here could build on its growing footprint in large-scale digital infrastructure lending, following its role in Meta Platforms' Hyperion data center financing, where it held $18 billion of debt at pricing and later recorded a $2 billion paper gain as valuations moved higher. That prior outcome may be shaping investor confidence as similar AI-linked projects come to market.
The Michigan buildout sits within a broader wave of sizable Oracle-backed data center financings, including $38 billion in debt tied to projects in Texas and Wisconsin, alongside another $18 billion for a New Mexico facility, highlighting the escalating capital intensity behind its cloud and AI expansion. At the same time, Oracle has appointed a new chief financial officer from Schneider Electric, a move that could help the company manage both funding requirements and execution risks as development accelerates. Taken together, the evolving financing discussions may signal that investor demand for AI infrastructure remains intact, even as deal sizes continue to push into increasingly large territory.
