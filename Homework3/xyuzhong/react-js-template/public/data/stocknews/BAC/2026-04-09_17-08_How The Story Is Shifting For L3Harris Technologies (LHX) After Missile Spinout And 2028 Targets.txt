L3Harris Technologies is back in focus as analysts update their price targets following fresh company guidance and portfolio news. Recent research ties these higher targets to the refreshed 2028 framework and the planned missile solutions spinout backed by a US$1b preferred equity investment from the Department of War, rather than short term trading factors. As you read on, you will see how to follow the evolving narrative, what analysts are watching, and where the biggest debate points sit for this name.
Analyst Price Targets don't always capture the full story. Head over to our Company Report to find new ways to value L3Harris Technologies.
Baird lifted its L3Harris Technologies price target to US$420 from US$385 after the investor day, pointing to 2028 targets that sit above current Street consensus and supporting a more constructive long term earnings framework.
BofA increased its target to US$400 from US$350 following the announced Missile Solutions spinout and US$1b preferred equity investment from the Department of War. The firm indicated that it sees scope for value to be reflected in the multiple after the reorganization.
Morgan Stanley reiterated an Overweight rating with a US$367 target, highlighting the planned missile solutions IPO and government investment as attractive for L3Harris shareholders and potentially a template for other large defense primes.
Citi, Truist, Bernstein, Seaport Research and others have also raised targets, often in the context of refreshed models that consider the new missile structure and the extended 2028 framework.
Even with higher targets, several research notes reference uncertainty around the broader defense sector and political backdrop, which can make execution against L3Harris longer term goals and valuation re rating less straightforward.

L3Harris and Xoople announced plans to co-develop a satellite constellation designed to serve AI era data needs, targeting high precision Earth observation customers in both enterprise and government markets.
L3Harris equipment supported the Artemis II mission, providing propulsion systems, in-space thrusters, avionics, and communications for the first crewed journey toward the moon in more than 50 years.
The Pentagon agreed to invest US$1b in L3Harris' Missile Solutions business, with plans to spin it off through an IPO in the second half of 2026 while L3Harris keeps a majority stake.
TechCrunch reported that a former head of L3Harris' Trenchant unit pleaded guilty to stealing and selling eight hacking tools to a Russian company serving government clients, with U.S. prosecutors citing harm to U.S. intelligence interests.
